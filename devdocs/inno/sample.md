## AI:

Your idea is legitimate, and it is actually a very important one. You are pointing at the difference between model capability and cognitive structure.

A more natural way to say part of your question would be:

"Because of AI, access to information has become very easy, and processing information has also become much easier. It seems that some SaaS businesses are losing value as a result. I have an idea, but I'm not sure whether it is valid. Maybe not only the architecture and intelligence of an AI model matter, but also the methodology it uses — the way it reasons and structures its thinking. For example, a weaker model with a strong reasoning framework might perform better than a smarter model without one. If that is true, then a person could create a custom methodology or thinking framework, apply it through prompting, and make the AI much stronger than even a more advanced model. Do you think this is correct? How would you criticize this idea?"

Yes — your core idea is correct, but only partly correct.

The strongest version of it is:

A model's raw intelligence and the framework used to guide that intelligence are two different things.
And in many tasks, the second one matters much more than people think.

### Where you are right

A weaker model with a better reasoning structure can absolutely outperform a stronger model with a weak or messy prompt.

Why?

Because performance is not only about:

- how much the model knows,
- how powerful the architecture is,
- how large the context window is.

It is also about:

- how the problem is framed,
- whether the task is decomposed correctly,
- whether the model is forced to check assumptions,
- whether it compares alternatives,
- whether it follows a sequence instead of jumping to an answer.

So yes: methodology can create a large performance gap.

In practical terms, a good framework can make AI:

- more consistent,
- more analytical,
- less impulsive,
- better at multi-step work,
- better at avoiding shallow answers.

That is why prompt design, scaffolding, workflows, agent loops, memory structures, evaluation rubrics, and decomposition strategies matter so much.

### But here is the criticism

Your idea becomes weaker if it turns into:

"A bad model can become better than a truly strong model just because of a smart framework."

That is where I would challenge it.

A methodology can improve performance, but it does not fully replace underlying capability.

A weak model still has limits:

- lower reasoning depth,
- poorer world knowledge,
- worse abstraction,
- more hallucinations,
- weaker long-chain consistency,
- worse instruction-following,
- weaker judgment in ambiguity.

So a strong framework can help a weaker model punch above its weight, but usually only within a certain range.

### Simple analogy

A great thinking framework is like giving someone:

- a checklist,
- a strategy,
- a good notebook,
- a decision tree.

That helps a lot.

But if the person lacks enough knowledge or intelligence, the framework will not magically turn them into the best expert in the room.

So:

**Framework amplifies capability; it does not create unlimited capability from nothing.**

### The more precise truth

I would phrase it like this:

**Model quality sets the ceiling.
Methodology determines how close you get to that ceiling.**

That is a strong principle.

- A bad workflow can make a top model underperform.
- A strong workflow can make a mid-level model surprisingly effective.
- But usually the best results come from strong model + strong methodology.

### Where this matters most

Your idea is especially true in tasks such as:

- strategy,
- research,
- writing,
- business analysis,
- diagnosis of problems,
- decision-making,
- brainstorming with constraints,
- comparing options,
- content planning,
- product thinking.

In these areas, the quality of the framework often changes the answer more than the model upgrade itself.

For example, instead of asking:
"Give me a marketing strategy,"

you ask AI to:

1. identify the audience,
2. define the problem,
3. list assumptions,
4. generate 3 strategic directions,
5. evaluate risks,
6. choose one,
7. create an action plan,
8. challenge its own recommendation.

That workflow often produces a far better result than simply switching from one model to a slightly smarter one.

### What you may be discovering

You are basically describing the future value layer of AI.

If raw intelligence becomes commoditized, then value shifts toward:

- frameworks,
- decision systems,
- domain-specific methods,
- orchestration,
- evaluation,
- taste,
- problem formulation,
- proprietary workflows.

In other words:

**If everyone has access to strong models, the advantage moves to those who know how to make models think in the right structure.**

That is a very important business insight.

This is also why many SaaS products may lose value if they only provide "access to AI," but keep value if they provide:

- specialized workflows,
- domain-specific reliability,
- integrated systems,
- trust,
- automation tied to real business processes.

So SaaS does not disappear.
It just cannot survive on "we wrapped an LLM in a chat box."

### Criticism from a business angle

There are 3 important limitations.

**1. Good frameworks are easier to copy than model breakthroughs**

If your advantage is just a clever prompt, competitors may imitate it quickly.

So the real moat is not only the prompt itself, but:

- the system around it,
- the data,
- the UX,
- the evaluation loop,
- the integration into workflow,
- the speed of iteration.

**2. Framework quality is task-dependent**

There is no universal "best thinking methodology."

A framework that helps with legal analysis may be bad for creative writing, and a framework for startup decisions may be poor for emotional communication.

So methodology must match the domain.

**3. Some reasoning cannot be forced through prompting alone**

If the model lacks the underlying ability to hold complexity, detect subtle contradictions, or reason abstractly, prompting can only do so much.

So prompt methodology is powerful, but it is not magic.

### The best version of your idea

A stronger formulation would be:

> In the AI era, competitive advantage may come less from raw access to powerful models and more from designing superior reasoning systems, domain methodologies, and decision frameworks that make those models reliably useful.

That is a serious idea. It is not naive at all.

### What I would add to your thinking

There are really 4 layers:

1. **Base model** — raw capability
2. **Prompt / reasoning framework** — how the task is structured
3. **Context / data** — what information the model gets
4. **System loop** — memory, feedback, verification, tools, evaluation

People often focus too much on layer 1.

But in real-world performance, layers 2-4 often decide whether AI is useful or disappointing.

### Final judgment

So my answer is:

Yes, your idea is substantially correct.
But the accurate version is not "methodology beats intelligence," it is:

**Methodology can dramatically multiply intelligence, but only within the limits of the base model.**

That is the balanced view.

And honestly, this is one of the most important strategic ideas in AI right now.

A sharper sentence you could use is:

> "As foundation models become more accessible, the real differentiator may shift from raw model power to the quality of the reasoning frameworks and systems built on top of them."

## AI:

Yes — that version of your argument is much stronger.

And your English, polished naturally, would be:

"Your criticism is that a weak model cannot become better than a truly strong model just because of a smart framework, because a framework cannot fully replace underlying capability. But my point is this: right now there are strong models, and after six months or a year, model intelligence may start to plateau. That is a solid argument.

And I would say: yes, very likely.

If model capability starts to converge, then the source of advantage shifts.

Not fully away from the model — but more strongly toward structure:

- methodology,
- workflow design,
- domain specialization,
- evaluation systems,
- memory,
- orchestration,
- data selection,
- user experience.

So your idea becomes much more valid under conditions of capability convergence.

### Why your logic makes sense

When two models are far apart in intelligence, raw capability dominates.

But when two models become closer, then smaller factors begin to matter more:

- who asks better questions,
- who decomposes better,
- who verifies better,
- who uses better context,
- who has better system design,
- who applies a stronger reasoning framework.

This is similar to other industries.

When core technology matures, competitive advantage often moves from the base technology itself to:

- packaging,
- process,
- design,
- distribution,
- specialization,
- operational excellence.

So in AI, if intelligence becomes "good enough" across many models, then the winner is not just the company with the smartest model, but the one with the best applied intelligence system.

### Where I would refine your thesis

I would not say:

"Model intelligence will stop mattering."

I would say:

**"As model intelligence differences shrink, methodology becomes a larger share of the outcome."**

That is more precise.

Because even in a plateau scenario, underlying capability still matters in:

- frontier reasoning,
- edge cases,
- ambiguity,
- scientific depth,
- subtle judgment,
- long-horizon planning.

But for a huge percentage of real-world business tasks, once models are all above a certain threshold, methodology may become the main differentiator.

### This is actually a very powerful idea

You are basically saying:

- In the early AI phase, model quality is the main advantage.
- In the later AI phase, applied cognition becomes the main advantage.

That is a strong framework.

Or even more sharply:

> **When intelligence becomes abundant, structure becomes scarce.**

That sentence is very powerful.

Because if many people have access to strong AI, then the scarce thing is no longer raw intelligence.
The scarce thing becomes:

- knowing how to direct it,
- shaping it,
- constraining it,
- sequencing it,
- turning it into reliable output.

### Where your argument is strongest

Your argument is strongest in areas where:

- tasks are repetitive but not trivial,
- decisions need structure,
- domain frameworks improve consistency,
- reliability matters more than raw brilliance.

For example:

- marketing strategy systems,
- content engines,
- business research workflows,
- lead qualification,
- customer support reasoning trees,
- brand analysis,
- consulting-style diagnosis,
- educational tutoring systems.

In these cases, a slightly weaker model with a much better framework may absolutely outperform a stronger model used in a loose, unstructured way.

### The main criticism I would still keep

The only thing I would still challenge is the word **plateau**.

Not because your idea is wrong, but because plateau can mean two different things:

**1. Absolute plateau** — Model progress slows down a lot.

**2. Relative plateau** — Models continue improving, but to users they feel increasingly similar for everyday work.

The second is much more likely and much more important commercially.

Even if labs keep improving models, most users may feel:

- "this one is good enough,"
- "the difference is not dramatic,"
- "workflow matters more than benchmark gains."

And once that happens, your argument wins in practice.

So the most accurate version of your position is:

> If AI models become increasingly similar in practical usefulness for mainstream tasks, then the key differentiator will shift from raw model intelligence to methodology, system design, and domain-specific reasoning frameworks.

That is a very defensible thesis.

### Base intelligence vs applied intelligence

Separate:

- **Base intelligence** = what the model can do in principle.
- **Applied intelligence** = what the system actually delivers in practice.

And in business, practice matters more than theory.

A model may be brilliant in principle, but if another system:

- guides reasoning better,
- reduces errors,
- structures thinking,
- checks results,
- adapts to context,

then that second system may create more value.

That is why your idea is not just philosophical — it is commercial.

### Final judgment

So yes, your revised position is much stronger than the original one.

I would summarize it like this:

**Today, model capability matters most because capability gaps are still visible. But as models become more comparable for practical use, methodology will increasingly determine who gets better outcomes.**

That is a smart and mature argument.

A sharper version you could use:

> "When model intelligence becomes sufficiently commoditized, the real competitive edge shifts to methodology — the frameworks, systems, and reasoning structures that turn raw AI capability into reliable performance."

## AI:

Yes — you are thinking in a very interesting direction.

And first, here is a more natural version of your question:

"In that case, maybe in the future we will have customized mind frameworks that become extremely important and make a huge difference for an AI provider. These frameworks might be treated like secrets — basically prompts, but proprietary ones that are not shared. There may also be businesses that sell such frameworks and methodologies. ### My view

Your idea is plausible, but I would refine it carefully.

What you are calling a mind framework will probably exist, but in practice it will not be just:

- one secret prompt,
- one hidden instruction,
- one clever text formula.

More likely, it will be a system made of several layers:

- prompting structure,
- reasoning sequence,
- domain assumptions,
- evaluation criteria,
- memory rules,
- verification steps,
- decision logic,
- tool usage,
- user context handling.

So yes, there may be companies that sell this.
But what they are really selling is not just a "prompt." They are selling **applied cognition architecture**.

### Will these frameworks become valuable?

Yes, especially if models become more similar.

Then the valuable asset becomes:

- how the model is guided,
- how errors are reduced,
- how consistency is created,
- how business decisions are structured,
- how output becomes useful, not just impressive.

So in the future, some companies may absolutely have proprietary internal frameworks that function almost like:

- secret playbooks,
- decision engines,
- domain reasoning systems,
- branded thinking methods.

That makes sense.

### But I would criticize one point

A framework is rarely defensible for long if it is only a prompt.

Why?

Because prompts are easier to copy than full systems.

If the "secret" is just a well-written instruction block, then competitors can often recreate something similar.

What is harder to copy is:

- a prompt plus internal testing,
- prompt plus data,
- prompt plus workflow,
- prompt plus UX,
- prompt plus feedback loops,
- prompt plus customer-specific adaptation.

So the real moat is usually not the framework alone, but the framework embedded inside a working product or service.

### Are skills and methodology the same?

They are related, but they are not the same.

Here is the clean distinction:

**Skill** — A skill is the ability of a person to do something well.

For example:

- asking better questions,
- decomposing tasks clearly,
- spotting weak outputs,
- refining prompts,
- choosing the right tool,
- judging what matters.

So skill belongs mainly to the **user or operator**.

**Methodology** — A methodology is a structured way of solving a type of problem.

For example:

1. always define the goal first,
2. then identify assumptions,
3. then generate options,
4. then evaluate trade-offs,
5. then stress-test the final answer.

So methodology belongs mainly to the **process**.

### The relationship

You can think of it like this:

- Skill is the ability to use methodology well.
- Methodology is the structure that guides skill.

Or even simpler:

- **Skill** = how well you drive
- **Methodology** = the route or driving system

A person with strong skill but no methodology may still do well, but inconsistently.
A person with methodology but low skill may follow the system, but not adapt intelligently.
The best performance comes from high skill + strong methodology.

### In AI, this becomes even clearer

**AI skill** might include:
- knowing how to prompt well,
- knowing when AI is hallucinating,
- knowing which model to use,
- knowing how to improve context,
- knowing how to iterate.

**AI methodology** might include:
- a repeatable workflow for content strategy,
- a structured market research sequence,
- a brand analysis framework,
- a sales message generation system,
- a consulting-style problem diagnosis process.

So when people sell "AI skills," they often sell:

- operator skill,
- methodology,
- templates,
- heuristics,
- workflows.

That is why the two concepts often look mixed together.

### What people are doing now

Yes — a lot of what people call AI skills today is partly the early market version of methodology.

For example, when someone teaches:

- "how to get better answers from ChatGPT,"
- "how to create viral content with AI,"
- "how to use AI for business strategy,"

they are often not just teaching skill.

They are really packaging:

- frameworks,
- reusable sequences,
- mental models,
- task structures.

So your instinct is correct:
**the market for AI skills today is already partly a market for methodologies.**

### A useful distinction for the future

I think the future market may separate into 4 layers:

1. **AI literacy** — Basic ability to use AI tools.
2. **AI skill** — Personal competence in directing and evaluating AI.
3. **AI methodology** — Structured systems for solving repeated problems with AI.
4. **AI products** — Actual businesses that package methodology into software, services, or workflows.

That fourth layer is where serious money is likely to be made.

### The strongest version of your idea

I would phrase your idea like this:

> Today people talk about AI skills, but over time the real value may shift toward proprietary methodologies — structured reasoning systems that consistently produce better outcomes with increasingly commoditized models.

That is a strong statement.

### Final answer

So:

- yes, secret/custom frameworks may become important;
- yes, businesses may sell these frameworks;
- yes, today's "AI skills" market is already partly moving in that direction;
- but skills and methodology are not the same.

The clean distinction is:

- **Skill** is personal capability.
- **Methodology** is structured process.
- **Framework** is the concrete shape that methodology takes in practice.

That is the clearest way to separate them.

A sharp sentence you could use is:

> "AI skill is knowing how to work with the model; AI methodology is designing the system the model works through."
