# Branch: Is Explore-and-Navigation One Underlying Operation (Concept Mapping + Content Consumption)?

## Question

The user pushed back on the prior inquiry's diagnosis (R2 vs Explore = Candidate E "separate but mechanism-sharing"; R3 vs Explore = Candidate F "specialization + composition") with three specific correction points:

1. **"r2 can be also run without SIC output as input"** — the existing /navigation discipline isn't strictly limited to completed SIC output; per `homegrown/navigation/SKILL.md`, it operates on "a completed SIC cycle (or current project state)" — the OR clause was under-weighted in the prior diagnosis.

2. **"r2 route-cards are sth probably that will be integrated to r3 concept nodes"** — R2's route-cards (Direction, Goal, Type, Priority, etc.) and R3's concept-nodes aren't structurally separate outputs; they look like different evolutions / different views of the same underlying object (a concept). The prior diagnosis treated them as separate output types.

3. **"explore is concept_mapping + consuming the underlying content. and actually navigation is the same."** — the deepest correction: Explore and Navigation share a SINGLE underlying operation — concept mapping + content consumption — and the surface differences (different inputs; different output schemas; different purposes) are evolutionary stages, not structural separateness.

**The question this inquiry must answer: is the user's unification claim correct? If yes, what is the structural truth (one operation, multiple instances; or one discipline mis-recognized as multiple), and what is the operational consequence (consolidate; rename; preserve current structure with shared-operation acknowledgment; or something else)? If no, where does the unification claim break down?**

## Goal

A structural diagnosis that handles the user's reframe honestly:

1. **Test the unification claim.** Does "concept mapping + consuming underlying content" actually describe Explore's operation? Does it describe R2's operation? Does it describe R3's operation? Apply structural-grounds testing to each.
2. **If the unification holds:** name the underlying operation explicitly; characterize what each surface instance (Explore, R2, R3) adds beyond the core operation; propose operational consequence.
3. **If the unification partially holds:** identify which parts unify and which parts don't; the answer may be "they share core operation X but also have non-shared add-ons Y and Z" rather than full unification or full separateness.
4. **If the unification doesn't hold:** explain structurally why the user's reframe is incorrect (this would require strong counter-evidence; would be the surprising answer given the user's confidence).
5. **Honor the prior diagnosis's structural observations** — the R2/R3 distinction; the assess overlay; the specialization. But re-examine whether those observations were correctly framed as STRUCTURAL SEPARATENESS or whether they're surface variations of the same underlying operation (the user's claim).
6. **Update the recommendation if needed.** The prior recommendation (Hybrid; fold R3 into Explore with naming preservation) may need revision if the unification claim changes the operational picture.

The user must be able to act on a revised verdict — either the prior diagnosis was substantially right (defend it against the unification claim), the prior diagnosis was partially right (refine), or the prior diagnosis was wrong in its framing (re-diagnose under unification).

## Candidate diagnoses to test (initial set; Exploration may extend)

- **Candidate A — Full unification.** Explore, R2, R3 are all instances of the same underlying operation ("concept mapping + content consumption"). Surface differences are evolutionary; no structural separateness. Project should recognize the unity explicitly (consolidate, rename, or refactor).
- **Candidate B — Partial unification.** All three share the core operation (concept mapping + content consumption) but also have NON-SHARED add-ons that ARE structurally meaningful. Diagnosis: "shared core + non-shared add-ons" — closer to the user's unification but preserves the prior inquiry's identification of distinct add-ons.

The unification claim conflates "shared mechanism" with "shared operation"; they're different concepts. Mechanism-sharing (both iterate; both produce maps) is real; operation-sharing (same underlying job) is not.
- **Candidate D — Unification at a higher abstraction level.** All three share the operation "produce structured representation of territory by consuming content"; but at the IMPLEMENTATION level they remain distinct because their schemas, inputs, and outputs are not interchangeable. The unification is conceptual; the implementations are separate.
